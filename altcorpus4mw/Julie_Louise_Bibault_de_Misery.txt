Julie Louise Bibault de Misery &&&&& Introduction &&&&& '''Julie Louise Bibault de Misery''' née '''de Chemault''' (1732-1804) was a French court official. She was the ''Première femme de Chambre'' to the queen of France between 1765 and 1786 (first to Marie Leczinska, then to Marie Antoinette). &&&&& &&&&& Life &&&&& Julie de Misery was the daughter of Jacques and Marguerite de Beaucousin and married the court official Charles François Bibault de Misery in 1750. &&&&& &&&&& She was named chamber maid to queen Marie Leczinska and succeeded her aunt Marie Marguerite Bibault as Première femme de Chambre in 1765; after the queen's death, she was transferred to the household of Marie Antoinette in 1770. Marie Antoinette called her as ''l'impératrice reine'' ('Empress Queen')  She was pointed out as a political intriguer at court and as the ally of Paul François de Quelen de la Vauguyon and an agent of the Jesuits and the Devots. &&&&& &&&&& In 1775, the office of Première femme de Chambre was split in four, between de Misery and the three deputies Henriette Campan, Marie-Élisabeth Thibaut and Quelpée La Borde Regnier de Jarjayes, who took turns serving: they were all still in service in 1792, when the queen's household was dissolved, then in supeervision of six chamber maids (femme du chambre). &&&&& &&&&& Julie de Misery was implicated in the Affair of the Diamond Necklace by Jeanne de Valois-Saint-Rémy, who claimed her to have been her contact at court. &&&&& &&&&& Fiction &&&&& She was portrayed in The Queen's Necklace by Alexandre Dumas. &&&&& &&&&& References &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&& &&&&&
